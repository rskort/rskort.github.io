# Miller indices

Miller indices \((hkl)\) label the orientation of a family of crystal planes. To obtain them, take a plane's intercepts with the crystallographic axes in lattice-constant units, invert those intercepts, and reduce the result to the smallest integers. An infinite intercept gives an index of zero.

For a cubic lattice, the vector \([hkl]\) is normal to the \((hkl)\) plane. Thus FCC(100), FCC(110), and FCC(111) cut the same bulk crystal in different directions and expose square, rectangular, and triangular surface nets.

Parentheses denote a plane, as in \((111)\); square brackets denote a direction, as in \([111]\). Braces such as \(\{111\}\) denote all symmetry-equivalent planes.

## Why the termination matters

Indices specify orientation, not every detail of a slab. A material can have multiple chemical terminations, reconstructions, or registries for the same \((hkl)\). Always report the composition, termination, cell, and slab thickness alongside the facet.
